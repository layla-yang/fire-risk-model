Should You Pay for the Wildfire Insurance?

Property: 759 Boulder Ct, Stateline, NV 89449  |  The decision: Pay $24,000/year for insurance, or accept the risk?  |  Generated 2026-06-19 05:10 UTC
Our recommendation: skip the insurance — but read on, because the reason matters
Paying for the insurance would cost you $360,000 over 15 years. Our best estimate of what you'd actually lose without it, averaged across thousands of possible futures, is around $28K to $139K. That means insurance costs roughly 4× to 18× more than the average loss it would cover. The HOA's decision to vote it down makes financial sense.

But there's still a 6% to 31% chance over 15 years that the building burns down completely and you lose your full $334,000 share. The question isn't really "is insurance a good deal?" — it isn't. The question is "could your household survive a $334,000 hit if it happened?"

Why each version changed the answer

  • The first version said the wildfire risk was tiny — and that's actually correct. The parcel is in a developed area where the official US fire risk maps show a very low chance of wildfire reaching this exact spot.
  • The better version caught the much bigger risk we'd missed: any fire starting inside one of the units (kitchen, electrical, etc.) that spreads to the whole building structure. This is roughly 230× more likely than a wildfire reaching the building. This was the most important finding.
  • The best version added climate warming. Surprisingly, it made things only slightly different — because the interior-fire risk doesn't depend on climate. So even though climate change makes wildfires worse, it barely moves our answer for this building.
  • The reality check against real insurance loss data showed our model is probably 5–12× too optimistic in absolute terms. We adjusted upward.

When you should reconsider this decision

  • Within 5 years: If another major wildfire (like Caldor 2021) happens within 30 miles of the building. Insurance loss data for the region will spike, and the math could change.
  • Within 5–7 years: If the building manager retires and the HOA's informal defenses lapse (sprinklers stop being tested, defensible space isn't cleared, etc.). Our analysis assumed mitigation is mostly maintained.
  • Anytime: If the master HOA insurance market re-opens at a lower price (under $15,000/year per owner). The current $24K is justified by today's market but expensive on the merits.
  • If your finances change: If a $334,000 loss would no longer be financially survivable for you (job change, divorce, etc.), insurance becomes a defensible purchase even if it's a bad deal on average.

What we don't know for sure

Every model is wrong in some way. Here's what could change our answer if it turned out differently than we assumed:

  1. How many units are in the building? We assumed somewhere between 4 and 16. If it's actually more (say 20+), the interior-fire risk goes up proportionally and the answer leans more toward insurance.
  2. How well-maintained is the building, really? We assumed mitigation is maintained in 70% of scenarios and degrades over time in 30%. If the HOA falls apart, the risk gets worse.
  3. Will the carrier raise prices? Real WUI premiums are rising 10–25% per year. The $24K could become $40K within a few years, which doesn't change our answer (it makes insurance even worse value), but it's worth tracking.
  4. Could climate change be worse than projected? We used the standard CMIP5 models. The newer CMIP6 models suggest slightly worse warming. But because interior fires dominate the risk for this specific building, this doesn't move the answer much.
  5. California data may not fully transfer to Nevada. Our most detailed data on what makes buildings burn comes from California's post-fire inspections (132,000 of them). Nevada fire physics is similar, but local building codes and wind patterns differ slightly.

The bottom line, in one paragraph

Skip the insurance. Over 15 years, you'd pay $360,000 in premiums to protect against an average loss of about $20,000–$83,000 — a bad deal on the math. The one situation where this advice is wrong: if a $334,000 loss in a single year would be genuinely devastating to your household finances. In that case, the insurance is overpriced but defensible as a hedge against an outcome you simply can't afford — that's what insurance is for. There's a 6–25% real-world chance of that happening over 15 years.
